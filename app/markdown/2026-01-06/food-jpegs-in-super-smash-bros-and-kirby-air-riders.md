# “Food JPEGs” in Super Smash Bros & Kirby Air Riders

<div class="row">
<div class="col-6">
<!-- more -->
<p>Have you ever noticed that the food graphics in Super Smash Bros.
and Kirby Air Riders are flat “<a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2.5D#Billboarding">billboarded</a>” stock images of food?
</p>
<p>This artistic decision from director <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Masahiro_Sakurai">Masahiro Sakurai</a> has persisted through 8 games over  nearly 25 years.
I've seen a few folks online remarking about the “JPEG” or “PNG”-like quality of the images in the most recent release: <nobr>Kirby Air Riders</nobr>.</p>
<p>While researching every game with this art style and all 150+ unique food images I ended up fixing wikis,
reviewing a seasonal KitKat flavor, and preserving an uncatalogued image of tempura soba.</p>
<!-- more -->
</div>
<div class="col-6">
<p style="text-align: center;">
<img style="max-width: 100%" src="https://storage.googleapis.com/sethmlarson-dev-static-assets/food-jpegs/burger-title.png">
<br><small><em>Burgers from <nobr>Super Smash Bros. Melee (2001)</nobr>, <nobr>Kirby Air Ride (2003)</nobr>, <nobr>Super Smash Bros. Brawl (2008)</nobr>, <nobr>Super Smash Bros. Ultimate (2018)</nobr>, and <nobr>Kirby Air Riders (2025)</nobr>.</em></small>
</p>
</div>
</div>

Masahiro Sakurai is the director for
every game on this list, so clearly this is his artistic decision.
[Super Smash Bros. Melee](#super-smash-bros-melee-2001) was the first game to contain this
food art style, published in 2001. This style was then repeated
in [Kirby Air Ride](#kirby-air-ride-2003) (2003), [Super Smash Bros. Brawl](#super-smash-bros-brawl-2008) (2008),
[Super Smash Bros. for 3DS and Wii U](#super-smash-bros-3ds-and-wii-u-2014) (2014), [Super Smash Bros.
Ultimate](#super-smash-bros-ultimate-2018) (2018), and most recently in [Kirby Air Riders](#kirby-air-riders-2025) (2025).

Credit to Nintendo, HAL Laboratories, SORA Ltd.,
and Bandai Namco Studios as developers and publishers of these games.
Artwork was sourced from the [Spriters Resource](https://www.spriters-resource.com/).

<div class="col-6">
<p><em>Where it all began!</em> Super Smash Bros. Melee for the GameCube started off with 28 distinct food items, often found
in “<a href="https://www.ssbwiki.com/Party_Ball">Party Balls</a>”. Each type of food had a different “<a href="https://www.ssbwiki.com/Food#List_of_food">nutritional value</a>” and “yumminess quotient”
according to the <a href="https://www.ssbwiki.com/Food#Trophies">in-game trophy</a> dedicated to the food items.
</p>
<p>
Melee included many foods specific to Japanese
cuisine, such as <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unagi">unagi</a> (eel), <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Omurice">omurice</a>, <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Soba">soba</a>, <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dango">dango</a>, and <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gy%C5%ABdon">gyūdon</a>.
I do distinctly remember growing up as a “culinarily sheltered” kid in the midwest
United States and not understanding what many of these food items
were.
</p>
</div>
</div>
<p>The original stock images of Super Smash Bros. Melee and the next game, Kirby Air Ride,
have been partially <a href="https://github.com/Render96/Render96Wiki/wiki/Super-Smash-Bros.-Melee/8cc401bc92ef46f3bc0df25d28b19b5126236f35">discovered and documented</a> by a group called “<a href="https://github.com/Render96/Render96Wiki/wiki/Video-Game-Texture-and-Audio-Matches">Render96</a>”.
The stock images are from a company called “<a href="https://www.sozaijiten.com/">Sozaijiten</a>”. Many of the food images come from Material Dictionary CDs <a href="https://github.com/Render96/Render96Wiki/wiki/Super-Smash-Bros.-Melee/8cc401bc92ef46f3bc0df25d28b19b5126236f35#material-dictionary-volume-14-vegetables--fruits"><nobr>Volume 14</nobr> (Vegetables & Fruits)</a>,
<a href="https://github.com/Render96/Render96Wiki/wiki/Super-Smash-Bros.-Melee/8cc401bc92ef46f3bc0df25d28b19b5126236f35#material-dictionary-volume-22-food--dishes"><nobr>Volume 22</nobr> (Food & Dishes)</a>,
and <a href="https://github.com/Render96/Render96Wiki/wiki/Super-Smash-Bros.-Melee/8cc401bc92ef46f3bc0df25d28b19b5126236f35#material-dictionary-volume-73-cooking-japanese-western--chinese"><nobr>Volume 73</nobr> (Cooking Japanese, Western, & Chinese)</a>.
The apple stock image in particular was re-used all the way through Super Smash Bros. Ultimate (2018).
The burger, milk, dango, and donut are still missing their primary source.
</p>

<div class="col-6">
<p>Kirby Air Ride for the GameCube had significantly fewer distinct food items (12)
compared to Melee and maintained many of the same food stock images
from Melee, including the apple, burger, chicken, curry, omurice, onigiri, and ramen.
Nigiri was included, but the image was changed from a sushi board to a plate.
</p>
<p>The stock images had their saturation increased and the black borders
around the images are thicker, sometimes 2-3 pixels instead of only 1 pixel for Melee.</p>
</div>

<p>
While researching the foods in Kirby Air Ride I discovered
a wiki description of a “tempura soba” item that I'd never
heard of and wasn't included in the <a href="https://www.spriters-resource.com/gamecube/kirbyairride/asset/83974/">Spriters Resource spritesheets</a>
for Kirby Air Ride. Turns out that this item was changed to
a “hotdog” in the NSTC-M and PAL releases of Kirby Air Ride.
</p>

<p>
I was unable to find a non-blurry image of the tempura soba sprite online,
so of course I had to preserve this sprite myself. I <a href="https://mastodon.social/@sethmlarson/115766037890533860">purchased</a>
a Japanese copy of Kirby Air Ride, dumped the ROM using the <a href="https://docs.flippydrive.com/backup.html">FlippyDrive Disc Backup Utility</a>, and ran
the ROM using <a href="https://dolphin-emu.org/">Dolphin</a> with “Dump Textures” mode enabled
to archive the sprite directly from the game.
</p>

<p>Super Smash Bros. Brawl for the Wii has only one more food item compared to Melee (29) and introduces 11 new food items including bread, cake, candy, chocolate, cookie, melon soda, parfait,
peaches, pie, pineapple, and steak.
</p>
<p>About half of the Japanese-specific foods from both Melee and Kirby Air Ride were replaced: curry, omurice, onigiri, and ramen.</p>

<p>The art is less saturated and more “realistic” which is in-line
with the rest of the game's art direction. The images lost their black outline, likely
to draw less attention to the “arcade-y” feel that the previous titles had
with food items.</p>
